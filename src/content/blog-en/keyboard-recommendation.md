## What Is a Mechanical Keyboard and Why Should You Get One?

### Key Characteristics

Mechanical keyboards feature **an independent switch (mechanism) under each key**. Here's how they compare to membrane keyboards:

| Feature | Mechanical | Membrane |
|:---|:---:|:---:|
| Typing Feel | **Crisp, satisfying** | Mushy |
| Durability | **50–100 million keystrokes** | 5–10 million keystrokes |
| Response Speed | **Fast** | Slightly slower |
| Customization | **Swappable switches & keycaps** | Generally not possible |
| Price | ¥8,000–¥50,000 | ¥1,000–¥5,000 |
| Noise | Ranges from clicky to silent | Quiet |
| Long-session Comfort | **Less fatiguing** | More tiring |

The upfront cost is higher, but with **5–10x the lifespan**, mechanical keyboards are a better long-term investment.

## Switch Types: A Buyer's Guide

The **switch (or "axis")** is the heart of a mechanical keyboard. The three main types differ in feel and sound.

### The Big Three Compared

| Switch Type | Feel | Sound | Actuation Force | Best For |
|:---|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---|
| **Red (Linear)** | Smooth, light | Quiet | 45g | Long typing sessions, office use |
| **Blue (Clicky)** | Crisp, tactile click | Loud | 50g | Those who love the typing sound |
| **Brown (Tactile)** | Gentle bump | Moderate | 45g | All-rounders, beginners |

### Recommended Switches by Use Case

- **Remote work / Office** → **Red** (silent red is ideal). Won't disturb video calls
- **Programming** → **Brown**. Gentle feedback helps with accurate typing
- **Gaming** → **Red**. Light actuation force enables fast key presses
- **Writing** → **Blue**. The satisfying click creates a rhythm that makes typing enjoyable

### Other Notable Switches

- **Silent Red (Pink)**: Quieter version of red. Best for office environments
- **Silver (Speed)**: Shallow actuation point, optimized for gaming
- **Topre (Electrostatic Capacitive)**: Not technically mechanical, but offers the finest typing feel available (used in HHKB and Realforce)

## Key Layout Guide

### Main Layouts

| Layout | Key Count | Numpad | Function Keys | Best For |
|:---|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---|
| Full-size (100%) | 108–109 | Yes | Yes | Heavy number input |
| Tenkeyless (80%) | 87–91 | No | Yes | Save desk space |
| 75% | 82–84 | No | Yes (compact) | Balance of function & size |
| 65% | 66–68 | No | Via Fn key | Minimalists |
| 60% | 61–64 | No | Via Fn key | Ultimate portability |

**For beginners, tenkeyless (80%) or 75% layouts are recommended.** If you don't need a numpad, the extra desk space and closer mouse positioning are significant ergonomic benefits.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

### Q. How long do mechanical keyboards last?

A. Typically **50–100 million keystrokes**. Even typing 8 hours daily, that's **10+ years** of use. That's 5–10x the lifespan of a membrane keyboard.

### Q. Will the typing noise bother others?

A. With **red or silent red switches**, the noise level is comparable to a membrane keyboard. Adding a **desk mat** further dampens the sound. If you type during video calls, silent red switches are the safe choice.

### Q. English layout or Japanese layout?

A. **It depends on your typing habits.** If you frequently use Japanese input, the Japanese layout is the safer choice. Many programmers prefer English layout for its more logical symbol key placement, but it takes time to adjust.

### Q. Wired or wireless?

A. **For a fixed desk setup, wired is simple and reliable.** **For multi-device switching or portability, wireless is more convenient.** For gaming, wired used to be the only serious option, but modern wireless tech (LIGHTSPEED, 2.4GHz) has virtually eliminated latency concerns.

### Q. Can I swap keycaps?

A. Most mechanical keyboards support **keycap swapping**. With Cherry MX-compatible switches, you have access to a vast market of third-party keycaps. Materials are mainly **PBT** (durable, textured) and **ABS** (smooth, affordable).
